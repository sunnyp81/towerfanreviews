The Vortx Air Pro makes an attractive pitch: a bladeless tower fan for roughly 40 pounds. Bladeless designs have been firmly in Dyson territory for years, so a budget version is an interesting proposition. The question is whether it delivers enough to be a genuine value pick, or whether the compromises make it hard to recommend.

## Design and build quality

The Vortx Air Pro has the visual language of a bladeless fan: a loop on a cylindrical base, no visible blades, a clean silhouette. At this price the materials are predictably lightweight plastic, and you can feel that when you move it around. It does not feel fragile, but it lacks the reassuring solidity of pricier rivals.

Assembly is simple: the loop clips onto the base in a few seconds, no tools required. The control panel on the unit itself is straightforward: power, oscillation and a speed dial. There is no remote, which is a real limitation when the fan is across the room. The base is compact enough to fit into a bedroom corner without taking up much floor space.

## Airflow and cooling

This is where budget bladeless fans almost always fall short, and the Vortx Air Pro is no exception. Airflow is noticeably softer than a bladed tower fan at the same price. On the highest setting it produces a steady, gentle stream rather than the assertive push you get from a full-size bladed model. Within a couple of metres it provides useful cooling, but at the far end of a medium-sized room it loses much of its effect.

Oscillation covers around 60 degrees, which is adequate for a bedroom but narrower than most conventional tower fans. For desk use or a small home office it is well suited. For cooling a living room, it will struggle.

## Noise

Here the bladeless design earns its keep. The Vortx Air Pro is noticeably quieter than bladed budget fans, particularly on the lower two settings. There is a soft hum from the motor, but none of the blade chop that makes cheap conventional fans irritating at night. If quiet operation in a small bedroom is your main priority, this is a genuine advantage over bladed rivals at a similar price.

On the top setting the motor noise increases, though it remains less intrusive than comparable bladed fans. Most users will find the middle setting comfortable to sleep alongside.

## Running costs

Tower fans are cheap to run and the Vortx Air Pro is no exception. At moderate settings it draws well under 30 watts, which at current UK energy rates works out to well under a penny per hour. Running it overnight adds only a few pence to your bill. There is no reason to worry about the energy cost of any tower fan.

## Features

The feature set is minimal. You get three speeds, 60-degree oscillation, and power via the on-unit button. There is no timer, no remote and no natural breeze mode. For 40 pounds, that is broadly in line with expectations, though the absence of any timer at all is noticeable when some bladed rivals at the same price include a basic auto-off.

If features matter, the MYLEK and Russell Hobbs tower fans both offer remote control and timers at a comparable price point and are worth comparing.

## Is the Vortx Air Pro worth it?

For a specific buyer, yes. If you want a bladeless design for safety or aesthetics, your room is small, and you do not need a remote, the Vortx Air Pro is a reasonable purchase at 40 pounds. You are not getting Dyson performance, but you are getting the core benefit of a bladeless fan: no exposed blades, easier cleaning, and quieter operation than budget bladed rivals.
