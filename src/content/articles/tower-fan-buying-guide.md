Tower fans look almost identical to one another, which makes choosing one weirdly difficult. This guide cuts through the marketing so you can match a fan to your home in a few minutes.

## Airflow and room size

The taller the fan, the higher it can push air over furniture, useful in living rooms. For a bedroom or study, a shorter fan aimed at the bed or desk is fine. Don't be swayed by "powerful" claims; oscillation coverage matters more than peak airflow for cooling a whole room.

## Oscillation

Oscillation is the side-to-side sweep that spreads cool air around rather than at one fixed spot. A wider angle (some reach 90° or more) keeps a whole sofa or bed comfortable. It's the single most useful feature on a tower fan.

## Noise

Noise is the most common regret. Check what a fan sounds like on its **lower** settings, because that's what you'll sleep with. A fan that's bearable on max is rare; a fan that's quiet on low is what you actually want. Bladeless and higher-end models tend to be the quietest.

## Timers, remotes and modes

A timer lets the fan switch off after you fall asleep. A remote saves getting up. Sleep or night modes dim the display and drop to quieter speeds automatically. These extras are cheap to add and make a fan far nicer to live with.

## Running costs

Tower fans are extremely cheap to run, typically 1-2p per hour. Energy use is not a reason to avoid them. For exact figures, see our [tower fan running cost guide](/tower-fan-running-cost/).

## Bladeless vs bladed

Bladeless fans are safer, easier to clean and usually quieter, but pricier. Bladed tower fans are cheaper and can move more raw air. Neither is "better", it depends on whether you value design and safety or value for money.
